\frametitle{What is situational awareness?}
\begin{description}
\item[Situational awareness] the ability to perceive and understand your environment, recognize potential hazards, and predict their impact.
\end{description}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[height=0.5\textheight]{Picture1}
\end{center}You can quickly act to stop any escalation and prevent harm to yourself or others.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{How Situational Awareness Training Boosts Workplace Safety}
There are many different types of cases in where you need to be aware of potential dangers or hazards:
\begin{itemize}
\item Aware of the current risks where a fire could start, midigating them
\item Aware of the evacuation route of the building you are in % during a fire emergency is an important life saving skill and is very crucial.
%% \item Leaving a frayed cord plugged into the wall, or leaving something flammable on a hot surface, having lighters or matches within children's reach, then choosing to not to remove the hazard is an example of taking an unnecessary risk.
\item Walking through a dangerous area alone or even just down a street at night. It is always good to pay attention to your surroundings, not just from possible criminal threats but to unseen hazards as well.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Fear vs Awareness}
\begin{columns}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
It is important to make a clear distinction of what is situational awareness or just a fear-based reaction:
\begin{itemize}
\item Constant state of heighten awareness exhausting; detract from other aspects life and work.
\item It is important to practice situational awareness from a calm and collected mindset. % The goal of situational awareness is to promote a safe work environment but being constantly on edge and afraid is also not safe.
\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{The O.O.D.A. Loop}
\framesubtitle{A 4 step decision making framework}
\begin{columns}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
\begin{description}
\item[Observe] Look around and see what is happening
\item[Orient] Put all the information in context
\item[Decide] Make your decision based on the information and context
\item[Act] Perform the identified task or action
\end{description}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.5\textwidth}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Picture3}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{The O.O.D.A. Loop in Action}
\begin{columns}
\begin{column}{0.6\textwidth}
You are driving home from work: you \emph{observe} the car in front of you that keeps veering to the side, braking and accelerating unexpectedly. You \emph{orient} (put all information into context) and suspect there could be possible intoxication involved. You \emph{decide} this is a potential hazard and to mitigate the risk by putting distance between you and the hazard. You \emph{act} by slow down, then call the authorities to notify them of a potential hazard with the driver so they can prevent an accident before it happens.
\end{column}
